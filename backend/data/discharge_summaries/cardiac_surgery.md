# Post-Operative Care: Coronary Artery Bypass Graft (CABG)

## Discharge Summary

### Procedure Overview
The patient underwent a coronary artery bypass graft (CABG) surgery. This procedure reroutes blood around blocked or narrowed coronary arteries to improve blood flow to the heart muscle.

### Medications

#### Prescribed Medications
- **Aspirin 81mg** — Take once daily with food. Do NOT stop taking this medication without consulting your cardiologist. This is critical for preventing graft clotting.
- **Metoprolol 25mg** — Take twice daily (morning and evening). This controls heart rate and blood pressure. Do not skip doses.
- **Atorvastatin 40mg** — Take once daily at bedtime. This manages cholesterol levels.
- **Lisinopril 10mg** — Take once daily. Helps control blood pressure and protects heart function.
- **Acetaminophen (Tylenol) 500mg** — Take every 6 hours as needed for mild pain. Maximum 3000mg per day.

#### Medication Warnings
- **DO NOT take ibuprofen (Advil/Motrin), naproxen (Aleve), or any other NSAIDs.** These can interfere with aspirin's blood-thinning effect and increase the risk of bleeding.
- **DO NOT take any new supplements** (including fish oil, vitamin E, or herbal supplements) without consulting your doctor, as these may interact with your blood thinners.
- If you miss a dose of Metoprolol, take it as soon as you remember unless it is close to your next dose. Never double up.

### Wound Care

#### Sternal Incision
- Keep the incision clean and dry for the first 2 weeks.
- Gently wash with mild soap and water daily after the first 48 hours. Pat dry with a clean towel.
- Do NOT apply lotions, creams, powders, or ointments to the incision unless prescribed.
- Monitor daily for signs of infection: redness spreading beyond 1 inch of the incision, increased warmth, swelling, drainage (especially if yellow/green or foul-smelling), or fever above 101°F (38.3°C).

#### Leg Incision (if saphenous vein graft was used)
- Same care instructions as the sternal incision.
- Some swelling in the leg is normal. Elevate your leg when sitting.
- Wear compression stockings as prescribed for 2-4 weeks.

### Activity Restrictions

#### First 2 Weeks
- No driving.
- No lifting anything heavier than 5 pounds (about the weight of a gallon of milk).
- Walk daily, starting with 5-10 minutes and gradually increasing by 5 minutes every few days.
- Use a sternal precaution: Do not push, pull, or reach above your head. Hold a small pillow against your chest when coughing or sneezing.

#### Weeks 2-6
- Gradually increase walking to 30 minutes daily.
- No heavy housework, vacuuming, or mowing the lawn.
- No sexual activity until cleared by your surgeon (typically at the 4-6 week follow-up).
- You may ride as a passenger in a car for short trips.

#### After 6 Weeks
- Your surgeon will assess readiness for cardiac rehabilitation.
- Driving may resume once cleared by your surgeon and you are off narcotic pain medication.

### Diet
- Follow a heart-healthy diet: low sodium (less than 2000mg/day), low saturated fat, high fiber.
- Eat 5-6 small meals rather than 3 large ones if appetite is poor.
- Drink 6-8 glasses of water daily unless fluid-restricted.
- Limit caffeine to 1-2 cups of coffee per day.
- Avoid alcohol for the first 2 weeks and limit afterward.

### Red Flags — Call 911 Immediately
- Chest pain or pressure that does not go away with rest
- Sudden shortness of breath or difficulty breathing
- Fainting or loss of consciousness
- Signs of stroke: sudden numbness/weakness on one side, slurred speech, vision changes
- Uncontrolled bleeding from incision

### Red Flags — Call Your Surgeon's Office
- Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
- Redness, swelling, or drainage from incision
- Rapid or irregular heartbeat
- Sudden weight gain of more than 3 pounds in 24 hours (may indicate fluid retention)
- Persistent nausea or vomiting
- Worsening leg swelling or pain (may indicate blood clot)

### Follow-Up Appointments
- Surgeon follow-up: 2 weeks post-discharge
- Cardiologist follow-up: 4 weeks post-discharge
- Cardiac rehabilitation assessment: 6 weeks post-discharge
