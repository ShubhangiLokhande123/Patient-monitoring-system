# Post-Operative Care: Total Hip / Knee Replacement (Arthroplasty)

## Discharge Summary

### Procedure Overview
The patient underwent a total joint replacement (arthroplasty). The damaged joint surfaces were replaced with prosthetic components to restore mobility and relieve pain.

### Medications

#### Prescribed Medications
- **Acetaminophen (Tylenol) 1000mg** — Take every 8 hours around the clock for the first week. This is your primary pain medication.
- **Oxycodone 5mg** — Take every 4-6 hours as needed for breakthrough pain. Use the minimum effective dose and try to transition off within 2 weeks.
- **Enoxaparin (Lovenox) 40mg injection** — Inject subcutaneously once daily for 14 days. This prevents blood clots. Do not miss doses.
- **Pantoprazole 40mg** — Take once daily before breakfast to protect the stomach while on pain medications.
- **Iron supplement (ferrous sulfate 325mg)** — Take once daily to support recovery from surgical blood loss. Take with vitamin C for better absorption.

#### Medication Warnings
- **Avoid ibuprofen and NSAIDs** for the first 2 weeks as they may increase bleeding risk while on blood thinners.
- **Do NOT drink alcohol** while taking opioid pain medication (Oxycodone). This combination can cause dangerous drowsiness and breathing problems.
- Opioid medications commonly cause constipation. Take a stool softener (Docusate 100mg twice daily) as needed.

### Wound Care
- The surgical dressing should remain in place for 48 hours after surgery.
- After 48 hours, gently clean the incision daily with mild soap and water.
- Keep the incision dry. Use waterproof dressing or cover with plastic wrap when showering.
- Do NOT soak in a bath, hot tub, or pool for 4 weeks.
- Monitor for signs of infection: redness spreading beyond the incision, warmth, increasing swelling, wound drainage, or fever above 101°F.
- Staples or sutures will be removed at your 2-week follow-up appointment.

### Activity & Mobility

#### Hip Replacement Specific Precautions
- **Hip precautions must be followed for 6-12 weeks** depending on surgical approach:
  - Do NOT bend your hip past 90 degrees (do not bend forward past your waist).
  - Do NOT cross your legs or ankles.
  - Do NOT twist or rotate your operated leg inward.
  - Use a raised toilet seat and shower chair as provided.
  - Sleep on your back or on the non-operated side with a pillow between your knees.

#### Knee Replacement Specific Precautions
- Perform prescribed range-of-motion exercises 3 times daily.
- Goal: achieve 90 degrees of knee flexion by week 6.
- Use ice packs for 20 minutes after exercises to manage swelling.
- Keep the leg elevated when resting.

#### General Mobility
- Use your walker or crutches as instructed until your physical therapist clears you.
- Walk regularly: start with short walks (5-10 minutes) every 2-3 hours while awake.
- Do NOT drive until cleared by your surgeon (typically 4-6 weeks).
- Avoid stairs if possible. If necessary, lead with the non-operated leg going down and the operated leg going up ("up with the good, down with the bad").

### Physical Therapy
- Home physical therapy will begin within 3-5 days of discharge.
- Sessions are typically 3 times per week for the first 4 weeks, then transition to outpatient PT.
- Perform all prescribed home exercises daily between PT sessions.
- Some pain during exercises is normal, but sharp or severe pain means you should stop and report it.

### Diet
- Resume a normal, balanced diet as tolerated.
- Increase protein intake (lean meats, eggs, dairy, beans) to support tissue healing.
- Eat high-fiber foods and drink plenty of water to prevent constipation (a common side effect of pain medications).

### Red Flags — Call 911 Immediately
- Sudden, severe chest pain or difficulty breathing (possible pulmonary embolism)
- Sudden severe swelling, pain, and redness in the calf (possible deep vein thrombosis)
- Fainting or loss of consciousness
- Signs of stroke

### Red Flags — Call Your Surgeon's Office
- Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
- Wound drainage that is increasing, discolored, or foul-smelling
- Increasing redness or warmth around the incision
- New or worsening pain not controlled by medications
- Inability to bear weight as previously instructed
- Persistent nausea or vomiting

### Follow-Up Appointments
- Surgeon follow-up with wound check: 2 weeks post-discharge
- Physical therapy reassessment: 4 weeks post-discharge
- Full surgical review: 6 weeks post-discharge
