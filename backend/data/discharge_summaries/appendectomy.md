# Post-Operative Care: Laparoscopic Appendectomy / Cholecystectomy

## Discharge Summary

### Procedure Overview
The patient underwent a laparoscopic surgery (appendectomy or cholecystectomy). Small incisions were made in the abdomen, and the appendix or gallbladder was removed using minimally invasive techniques.

### Medications

#### Prescribed Medications
- **Acetaminophen (Tylenol) 500mg** — Take every 6 hours as needed for pain. Maximum 3000mg per day.
- **Ibuprofen (Advil) 400mg** — Take every 8 hours as needed for pain and inflammation. Take with food.
- **Ondansetron (Zofran) 4mg** — Take every 8 hours as needed for nausea (common after anesthesia for the first 24-48 hours).

#### Medication Notes
- Most patients manage pain well with over-the-counter medications after laparoscopic surgery.
- If pain is not adequately controlled, contact your surgeon's office before seeking additional medications.
- Antibiotics were given during surgery. You do NOT need to take antibiotics at home unless specifically prescribed.

### Wound Care
- You will have 3-4 small incisions (each about 0.5-1 cm).
- Steri-strips (butterfly bandages) or surgical glue may be covering the incisions. Allow these to fall off on their own (typically 7-10 days).
- Keep incisions clean and dry for the first 24 hours.
- After 24 hours, you may shower. Let water run over the incisions but do not scrub.
- Do NOT soak in a bath, pool, or hot tub for 2 weeks.
- Some bruising around incisions is normal.
- A small amount of clear or slightly pink drainage is normal for the first 24-48 hours.

### Activity & Recovery

#### First 48 Hours
- Rest at home. Mild fatigue and grogginess from anesthesia are normal.
- Walk around the house regularly to prevent blood clots and improve gas pain.
- You may experience shoulder pain — this is caused by residual CO2 gas used during surgery and will resolve within 48-72 hours. Walking helps it dissipate faster.

#### Days 3-7
- Gradually increase activity as tolerated.
- Most patients return to desk work or light duties within 3-5 days.
- Avoid heavy lifting (nothing over 15 pounds) for 2 weeks.
- Light walking and gentle stretching are encouraged.

#### After 2 Weeks
- Most activity restrictions are lifted at the 2-week follow-up.
- Resume exercise gradually. Listen to your body.

### Diet

#### After Appendectomy
- Resume a normal diet as tolerated. Start with bland, easy-to-digest foods (toast, rice, bananas, broth) and progress as comfort allows.
- Stay well hydrated. Drink at least 6-8 glasses of water daily.

#### After Cholecystectomy
- Start with a low-fat diet for the first 2-4 weeks. Your body needs time to adjust to digesting fats without a gallbladder.
- Avoid greasy, fried, or high-fat foods initially. These may cause diarrhea or stomach upset.
- Gradually reintroduce normal foods over 4-6 weeks.
- If you experience persistent diarrhea after fatty meals lasting more than 4 weeks, contact your surgeon.

### Red Flags — Call 911 Immediately
- Sudden severe abdominal pain that is getting worse, not better
- Fever above 102°F (38.9°C)
- Uncontrollable vomiting (unable to keep any fluids down for more than 12 hours)
- Signs of internal bleeding: feeling faint, rapid heartbeat, or vomiting blood

### Red Flags — Call Your Surgeon's Office
- Fever above 101°F (38.3°C)
- Increasing redness, swelling, or drainage from any incision
- Pain that is getting worse after the first 2-3 days (it should be improving)
- Persistent nausea or vomiting beyond 48 hours
- No bowel movement for 3 or more days after surgery
- Yellow discoloration of skin or eyes (jaundice) — especially after cholecystectomy

### Follow-Up Appointments
- Surgeon follow-up: 2 weeks post-discharge
- Call for earlier appointment if any concerns arise
